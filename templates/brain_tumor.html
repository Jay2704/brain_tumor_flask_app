About Brain Tumors

Brain tumors are abnormal growths of cells within the brain or central nervous system. They can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). Tumors can develop within the brain (**primary brain tumors**) or spread from other parts of the body (**metastatic brain tumors**).

Common Symptoms of Brain Tumors

Symptoms vary depending on the size, type, and location of the tumor. Common signs include:

  • Persistent headaches, often worse in the morning
  • Seizures or convulsions
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Weakness or numbness in specific body parts
  • Vision or speech difficulties
  • Cognitive or personality changes

Types of Brain Tumors

Meningioma

Meningiomas arise from the meninges, the protective membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord. They account for about 37% of all brain tumors. Most are benign, but larger tumors can press on brain tissue and cause complications.

Symptoms: Headaches, weakness or numbness in limbs, memory difficulties, and vision changes.

Treatment: Observation, surgery, or radiation therapy depending on the size and symptoms.

Glioma

Gliomas originate in the glial cells, which support and protect neurons in the brain. Gliomas include subtypes such as astrocytomas, oligodendrogliomas, and glioblastomas. These tumors can range from slow-growing to highly aggressive.

Symptoms: Seizures, headaches, cognitive changes, and vision problems.

Treatment: Surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy, often combined for aggressive forms like glioblastoma.

Pituitary Tumors

Pituitary tumors develop in the pituitary gland, a pea-shaped organ at the base of the brain responsible for hormone regulation. These tumors can be hormone-producing (functional) or non-functional. Functional tumors can lead to hormone imbalances.

Symptoms: Hormonal imbalances, vision problems, fatigue, weight changes, and mood swings.

Treatment: Medications to regulate hormones, surgery, and radiation therapy.

Diagnosis of Brain Tumors

Brain tumors are diagnosed using a combination of imaging tests, neurological exams, and biopsies. Common diagnostic tools include:

  • MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging): Provides detailed images of the brain to identify abnormal growths.
  • CT Scans: Helps detect tumors and evaluate swelling in the brain.
  • Biopsy: Involves removing a sample of tissue for laboratory analysis to determine the tumor type.

Treatment Options

Treatment varies based on the type, location, and stage of the tumor:

  • Surgery: Removing as much of the tumor as possible while preserving brain function.
  • Radiation Therapy: Targeted radiation to destroy tumor cells or reduce tumor size.
  • Chemotherapy: Drugs used to kill or slow the growth of tumor cells.
  • Targeted Therapy: Medications designed to target specific mutations in tumor cells.
  • Observation: For benign tumors that do not cause symptoms, regular monitoring may be sufficient.
